title: Getting your deposit back at move-out
---
When your lease ends, your landlord works through a deposit return in GAM to settle what you get back. Here's what happens and what to expect.

## How the math works

GAM starts with the deposit you paid (plus any interest that applies to your lease) and subtracts the deductions your landlord enters. What's left is your refund. If the deductions add up to more than your deposit, there's no refund and instead a balance you owe. The deductions can include:

- **Cleaning fees** — any move-out cleaning fee written into your lease is pulled in automatically.
- **Damage charges** — itemized lines your landlord adds for damage beyond normal wear. Each line has a description and amount.
- **Other deductions** — a catch-all your landlord can use for things like final utilities.
- **Unpaid balances** — any rent, utility, late fees, or other charges on your account that were still unpaid or had a failed payment get swept in and settled out of your deposit.

Because unpaid items roll in automatically, the cleanest way to protect your refund is to keep your balance at zero before you leave. If you pay off an outstanding charge before your landlord finalizes, it drops out of the deduction list.

## The three outcomes

1. **Full or partial refund** — the refund shows up on your Payments tab as a deposit credit and is paid back to you once your landlord's payout processes.
2. **Nothing owed either way** — deductions exactly used up your deposit.
3. **You owe a balance** — deductions came to more than your deposit. GAM records the difference and may automatically charge the payment method you have on file. If that charge doesn't go through, the balance stays open for your landlord to follow up on.

## Timeline and disputes

The deposit return is finalized by your landlord, not by GAM, and the time allowed for returning a deposit depends on your lease and your local laws — check those for the deadline that applies to you. GAM provides the tools and keeps the record; it doesn't decide deductions or referee disagreements. If a charge looks wrong, raise it with your landlord, and use the itemized lines in your account as your reference point.
